Different Types Of Motherboards

AT Motherboard

AT motherboard stands for Advanced Technical motherboard. The full-size AT board first debuted in August 1984, when IBM introduced the Personal Computer AT. It had a bulky design with limited connectivity and functionality, and had poor airflow. Since 1997, ATX has mainly replaced the AT form factor.

ATX Motherboard

The essential features of the LPX and Baby-AT motherboard designs are combined to create the ATX form factor. In July 1995, Intel first published the official ATX specification. It added new features and improvements which includes increased ventilation, standardized power connectors, and a newer layout for easier installation and future upgrades. ATX includes a variety of sizes such as standard, extended, micro, and mini.

BTX Motherboard

In September 2003, Intel published a motherboard form factor specification called Balanced Technology Extended (BTX). This form factor comes in different sizes such as the standard and pico sized. This motherboard was created to address the requirements for cooling and power and was supposed to be the successor of ATX. Although, the need for BTX has decreased due to the recent trend toward multicore processor designs that use less power.

ITX Motherboard

The ITX was first released in 2001 by VIA Technologies and was mainly made for small form-factor computers. Because ITX comes in small size, they are perfect for creating systems that are compact and require minimal power consumption, cooling effectiveness, and space. But when compared to ATX, they have fewer ports, expansion slots, and RAM slots because of their compact form size.

LPX Motherboard

The first LPX and mini-LPX form factor boards were created in 1987 by Western Digital. They had a small form factor, a riser card for expansion, and a low-profile design. This makes the system smaller than the Baby-AT which enables a slim or low-profile case design.
